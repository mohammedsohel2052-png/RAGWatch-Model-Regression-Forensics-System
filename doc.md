## What is a Deductible?

A deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance coverage kicks in. For example, if your policy has a $500 deductible and you file a claim for $2,000 in damages, you pay the first $500 and your insurer covers the remaining $1,500. Deductibles help reduce the number of small, frivolous claims and lower your premium costs. Higher deductibles generally mean lower monthly premiums, while lower deductibles mean higher premiums. Deductibles reset annually unless stated otherwise in your policy.

## What is a Premium?

A premium is the amount you pay to maintain your insurance policy, typically on a monthly or annual basis. Premiums are calculated based on risk factors including your age, location, claims history, credit score, and the coverage amount you select. Paying your premium on time is essential — a lapse in payment can result in policy cancellation and a gap in coverage. Some insurers offer discounts for bundling multiple policies, paying annually upfront, or maintaining a clean claims history.

## What is Covered Under Comprehensive Auto Insurance?

Comprehensive auto insurance covers damage to your vehicle from events that are not collisions. This includes theft, vandalism, natural disasters (hail, floods, hurricanes), fire, falling objects (tree branches, debris), and animal collisions (deer strikes). It does NOT cover collision damage (car accidents with another vehicle or object), medical bills, or the other driver's vehicle. Comprehensive coverage is optional unless your lender requires it. You choose your own deductible, which affects your premium.

## What is Liability Insurance?

Liability insurance covers the costs when you are legally responsible for injuring someone or damaging their property. In auto insurance, it has two components: Bodily Injury Liability (pays for medical expenses, lost wages, and legal costs for others you injure) and Property Damage Liability (pays for repairs or replacement of the other party's property). Liability insurance does NOT cover your own injuries or vehicle damage. It is the minimum legally required coverage in most states. Policy limits are expressed as split limits (e.g., 25/50/25) or a single combined limit.

## What Happens When You File a Claim?

Filing a claim begins when you contact your insurer to report a covered loss. The process includes: (1) Notifying your insurer promptly after the incident, (2) Providing documentation such as police reports, photos, and witness statements, (3) Working with an assigned claims adjuster who investigates and estimates the damage, (4) Receiving a payout minus your deductible once the claim is approved. Filing multiple claims in a short period can increase your future premiums. Some policies have a "claims-free" discount that resets if you file a claim.

## What is Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage?

Uninsured Motorist (UM) coverage protects you if you are involved in an accident with a driver who has no insurance. Underinsured Motorist (UIM) coverage applies when the at-fault driver has insurance, but their policy limits are too low to fully cover your damages. These coverages pay for your medical expenses, lost wages, and sometimes pain and suffering. UM/UIM coverage is required in some states and optional in others. It is generally inexpensive to add and is highly recommended given the significant number of uninsured drivers on the road.

## What is a Policy Exclusion?

A policy exclusion is a specific condition, circumstance, or event that is NOT covered by your insurance policy. Common exclusions in auto insurance include: intentional damage, racing or track events, using your personal vehicle for commercial delivery purposes (without a commercial rider), mechanical breakdown, and wear and tear. Exclusions are listed explicitly in your policy document. It is critical to read and understand all exclusions before purchasing a policy to avoid surprises when filing a claim. Some exclusions can be removed by purchasing additional riders or endorsements.

## What is the Difference Between Collision and Comprehensive Coverage?

Collision coverage pays for damage to YOUR vehicle resulting from a collision — hitting another car, a guardrail, a fence, or rolling your vehicle, regardless of fault. Comprehensive coverage pays for damage to your vehicle from NON-collision events such as theft, fire, weather events, and animal strikes. Both have separate deductibles. A simple rule: if your car hits something or something hits your car (another vehicle, a pole), that is collision. If the damage is caused by nature, theft, or anything other than a direct collision, that is comprehensive. Many lenders require both coverages on financed vehicles.

## What is an Insurance Rider or Endorsement?

A rider (also called an endorsement) is an optional add-on to your standard insurance policy that provides additional coverage beyond what the base policy covers. Common auto insurance riders include: Roadside Assistance (towing, flat tire, battery jump), Rental Car Reimbursement (pays for a rental while your car is being repaired), Gap Insurance (covers the difference between your car's current market value and the remaining loan balance if your car is totaled), and New Car Replacement (pays for a brand-new car rather than the depreciated value). Riders increase your premium but can provide significant protection in specific scenarios.

## How Are Insurance Premiums Calculated?

Insurance companies use actuarial data and risk modeling to calculate your premium. Key factors include: your driving record (accidents, tickets, DUIs significantly raise premiums), age and gender (young male drivers typically pay more), vehicle make and model (sports cars and luxury vehicles cost more to insure), annual mileage (more miles driven means more risk), geographic location (urban areas with higher theft and accident rates cost more), credit score (in most states, lower credit scores lead to higher premiums), and coverage level selected. Insurers re-evaluate your risk profile at each renewal period. Shopping around and comparing quotes from multiple insurers is always recommended.
